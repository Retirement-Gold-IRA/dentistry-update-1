COVID-19 pandemic has impacted dental care

Since the outbreak of the HIV pandemic in the 1980s, dental professionals have increased their infection control practices. With close proximity to patients who may be infected with COVID-19, oral health professionals face a unique set of challenges. Additionally, oral health professionals often need to perform procedures unmasked. In addition, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration has classified aerosol-generating procedures performed on patients with COVID-19 as a "high risk" for transmission of the virus.

The COVID-19 pandemic has had a dramatic impact on the oral health care industry. The epidemic disrupted many health systems and severely curtailed dental appointments. In addition, the number of patients seeking appointments was significantly increased. However, it is important to understand the specific effects of COVID on dental care.

Because the COVID-19 pandemic has caused a shortage of dental practitioners, preventive dental care has become less common. As a result, patients are often forced to visit emergency departments for their dental problems. In addition, many schools have suspended their oral health programs in response to the COVID-19 epidemic.

OSHA's guidance for healthcare personnel

OSHA recently released guidance for employers and healthcare personnel in the dental industry. This document outlines the requirements for dental employers and healthcare personnel, including how to assess the risk of exposures and select appropriate controls. The guidance also explains how to continually adapt control measures as risk levels and practices change.

To protect workers from aerosol exposures, OSHA recommends using a combination of standard precautions and personal protective equipment. These include eye protection and respiratory protection. These measures are necessary to protect dental workers who use aerosol-generating instruments or equipment. Examples include air-water syringes, ultrasonic scalers, and dental turbines.

Employers should consider the risk of SARS-CoV-2 infection in their health hazard assessments. SARS-CoV-2 can be transmitted from pre-symptomatic to symptomatic individuals and is spread by contact with an aerosol-generating device. In these circumstances, employers should consider additional protective measures, such as N95 respirators, to protect workers from potentially infectious materials.

Digital x-rays

Digital X-rays are used in dentistry to diagnose symptoms and determine the extent of dental injuries. They are also used to help dentists plan treatments for specific conditions. In some cases, dentists will use digital X-rays for orthodontic treatment or dental implant placement. Here's how they work.

Digital x-rays use less radiation than traditional x-rays. This means less radiation exposure for patients and dental professionals. They also are more user-friendly, with a computer screen that displays images immediately. Also, they don't require film development. Patients can see the images immediately and can get a better understanding of what's going on with their teeth.

Digital x-rays use an electronic sensor to project the image onto a dentist's computer, instead of film. Traditional x-rays are printed on film and must be developed before they can be used, which can delay diagnosis. The advantage of digital radiography is that it eliminates this delay, and projects an image as soon as the sensor is placed in the mouth.

Staying on top of your game

Staying on top of your game in dentistry means always striving to learn and improve. It's important to never let your success get to your head, and to remember to always have your patients' best interest in mind. It also means always remembering that the best dentists are the ones who are constantly improving.

Changing customer expectations and addressing their concerns are important parts of staying on top of your game in dentistry. Today, this means implementing new safety procedures, making sure your patients are comfortable and knowledgeable, and tracking your patients' annual exams and regular cleanings. In addition to keeping up with the latest trends, it also means keeping up with the latest technology, and implementing new safety measures.

Ask patients for their opinion. Then, make sure to take what they say to heart. Another helpful tip is to associate with a variety of different types of dental colleagues, including those with different skill sets and practice sizes. It's essential to have a broad understanding of what each position on the team does, and to be aware of their needs and concerns.
